Science Nuggets

Raindrops in the Solar Corona

Scientists from the U.S. National Science Foundation National Solar Observatory and New Jersey Institute of Technology produced the finest images of the Sun's corona to date. To make these high-resolution images and movies, the team developed a new coronal adaptive optics system that removes blur from images caused by the Earth's atmosphere. Their ground-breaking results were recently published in Nature Astronomy and pave the way for deeper insight into coronal heating, solar eruptions, and space weather, and open an opportunity for new discoveries in the Sun's atmosphere.

Published May 2025 — Dirk Schmidt, Thomas A. Schad, Vasyl Yurchyshyn, Nicolas Gorceix, Thomas R. Rimmele, Philip R. Goode

Death of a Sunspot

The interaction between magnetic fields and convection in sunspots during their decay process remains poorly understood. The high spatial and temporal resolution of the GST instruments allowed analysis of the causes of sunspot decay. Authors found that the area and total magnetic flux display an exponential decrease over the course of the sunspot decay, and identified 22 moving magnetic features in the moats of pores, a signature of sunspot decay through diffusion.

Published June 2023 — Astron. Astrophys., 686, id.A75

Magnetic Fields above Sunspot Light Bridges

High spatial-resolution NIRIS magnetograms combined with HMI magnetograms and nonlinear force-free field models to study magnetic structures associated with sunspot light bridges. The effort revealed a field-free region and different velocity structures inside the two light bridges. Analysis showed a low-lying magnetic canopy and enhanced current system above the light bridges.

Published August 2022 — Astrophys. J. Lett., 935, L27

Are Switchbacks produced by the Chromospheric Network?

We tested recent claims that the magnetic switchbacks detected by the Parker Solar Probe carry information on the scales of solar supergranulation and granulation using high-resolution H-alpha images from the GST. Spicules standing along a chromospheric network near the coronal hole boundary present similar scales to the medium scale of switchbacks, and the typical network diameter of 0.6-1.8 degrees suggests the medium scale can be understood as an equilibrium distance between flux tubes in solar wind.

Published August 2022 — Jeongwoo Lee et al., Astrophys. J. Lett., 935, L27

Kilogauss Magnetic Fields on the Sun

Line-of-sight magnetic fields and magnetic power spectra of a quiet Sun region were analyzed using HMI and BBSO NIRIS data. The NIRIS data revealed the presence of thin flux tubes of 200-400 km in diameter and field strength of 1000-2000 G. The HMI power spectra exhibited the same spectral index of -1 on a broad range of spatial scales, implying the same mechanism of magnetic field generation operates everywhere in the undisturbed photosphere.

Published March 2022 — Astrophys. J., 928, 41
